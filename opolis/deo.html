user problem

There are two types of employment in the US, W2, and 1099. As a full time employee (w2), you are eligible for a whole host of benefits provided or partially provided by the employer. As a contractor, aka freelancer (1099), you are not eligible for these benefits. The general thinking is that the hourly fee for a freelancer goes up to cover the costs of these benefits (health, insurance, 401k, etc), and all else stays the same. In reality, freelancers actually end up paying more for less good benefits simply because the networks which corporations are a part of drive down the cost. Freelancers don’t have access to these networks, and are therefore left spending more money and more time finding good health benefits, community and social safety nets. The disadvantages of freelancing are often larger than the advantages, yet freelancing continues to grow yearly, with even large corporations expressing interest in converting their entire workforce to contracting positions.

user research (validate user problem and document)

With no resources to dedicate to validation and user research, I had to get creative. To begin with, I interviewed the stakeholders in the company, documenting their responses and created a project brief. A starting point, surely, but I still wanted to validate the problem with real users. I got lucky and found a group to help with my research. During Denver Startup Week in 2018, I attended a group event called Better Together, who’s soul purpose was to find out what freelancers needed and why. The data and research that came out of that event was tremendous for my research purposes, and can be found here. Feeling good about validating the user problem, I progressed to researching a solution.

problem solving research (validate solutions and document) - aka, how to solve this problem

Blockchain, and it’s application on employment is a very novel idea, never done before with no historical projects on which to draw inspiration. How then, does one approach a problem like this? While the entire problem is novel, some of its individual parts are not. Starting with Precedent Analysis, I curated a list of companies which approached similar problems and documented their user experiences, including Coinbase, Aragon, DaoStack, Upwork, And.co, Honeybook, Freelancers Union, Colorado Connect4Health Insurance Marketplace, and others.

A part of figuring out this complicated system was participating in crucial discussions around Business Logic, as these would have a profound impact on the user experience and development. As a liaison between development and leadership, as well as singularly in charge of all things design, it was important to be a part of and contribute to these conversations and decisions.

user personas, flows, & red routes

Based on my research I produced user personas, which helped to validate and drive the Red Routes, requirements and functionality list, user flows, and ultimately the wireframes. The most important part of this process was curating the potential list of features into an MVP and getting leadership buy in on the requirements and my proposed timeline. The understanding of user needs directly translated into prioritizing functionality, developing an MVP, and pushing forward with curated objectives. This significantly reduced the features originally envisioned, however it also significantly increased the likelihood of success of the product.

wireframes

I began to ideate low level wireframes for onboarding users and a dashboard, as well as landing pages and a new homepage through sketches. I collaborated these low level sketches with development and then moved to low-fi Sketch mockups which I validated with users, stakeholders, and the wider team. A few iterations of this helped drive better low-fi wireframes, which I eventually converted into high fidelity mockups.
